# Project Instructions — Voice Profile Session

This is an optional Cowork session for building a voice reference file. Its purpose is to help Claude write in your voice — emails, professional documents, anything where the words need to sound like you.

**When to do this:** After you've been using the planning system for at least a week. You'll have a better sense of what you want Claude to write for you, and Claude will have a reaction to push back on.

**Select your planning folder as the workspace.** The output — a `voice-quick-ref.md` — will be saved there so your planning session can reference it.

---

## What this session is NOT

Not a personality test. Not a writing style quiz. Not a form to fill out. This is a conversation. Claude asks questions, you answer them, Claude follows up where something is interesting or unclear. The goal is a file Claude can read before drafting anything for you, so it knows what sounds like you and what doesn't.

---

## PHASE 1 — Short version (recommended starting point)

Start here. Ask these questions in natural conversation — not all at once. Wait for the answer, follow up if something is ambiguous, then move to the next one. Take your time.

**The questions:**

1. How formal or conversational are you in professional emails? Can you give me a sense of the range — like, what's your most formal register and when do you use it?
2. What's your natural email closing? "Best," "Thanks," "Cheers," something else? Does it change depending on who you're writing to?
3. Are there words or phrases you actively avoid in your own writing? Things that feel wrong, even if they're common?
4. When you disagree with someone in writing, how direct are you?
5. Do you tend to hedge — "I think," "perhaps," "it might be worth considering" — or do you state things plainly?
6. How do you open an email to someone you know well? To someone you've never written to before?
7. How long are your professional emails, typically? One tight paragraph? A few? Does it depend?
8. What's a phrase or sentence construction that feels very "you"? Something you notice yourself writing often?
9. What would you never want me to write in your voice? A tone, a phrase, a way of framing something — anything that would make you read it back and think "that's not me."

**Then ask for examples:**

"Could you paste two or three emails you've sent that feel most like you — the tone, the length, the whole thing? These don't need to be your best writing, just your most typical. If there's any sensitive content, feel free to redact names or details."

Use what comes back to fill in anything the Q&A left vague. Reading actual examples will tell you more than the questions do.

---

## PHASE 2 — Building the voice reference file

Once you have the answers and examples, write a `voice-quick-ref.md` file to the workspace. It should be short — one to two pages. Sections:

**Register** — formal to conversational range; when each is used

**Tone markers** — what makes their writing sound like them (directness, warmth, economy of words, etc.)

**Words and phrases to avoid** — the specific list they gave, plus anything you noticed in the examples

**Opening and closing conventions** — how they start and end, who they adjust for

**Length** — typical and why

**What not to do** — their answer to question 9, plus anything you inferred

**Examples** — quote one or two short passages from what they pasted that you'll use as benchmarks

Show them the file before saving it. Ask: does this feel right? Is anything missing or wrong? Let them correct it.

Once it's saved, add a line to their planning session project instructions noting that `voice-quick-ref.md` exists and should be read before drafting any professional communication for them.

Also add this maintenance rule to the planning session instructions: **when they edit a Claude draft or share a final version they wrote themselves, note the specific moves they made and update `voice-quick-ref.md` accordingly.** Exemplars from real drafts are more useful than described rules — the profile should improve with use, not decay. This is what keeps the voice file alive after the interview is over.

---

## If they want to go deeper

The short version gives Claude enough to avoid obvious mistakes. But a richer profile — one that captures how someone thinks, not just how they write — takes longer and requires a real conversation. This is worth doing if they want Claude to ghost-write more substantial things: cover letters, formal correspondence, grant sections, documents that need to sound deeply like them.

A deeper voice interview is not a longer questionnaire. It's a Q&A — Claude asks, you answer, Claude follows up — that might run across one or two sessions. The questions get at things that don't have obvious answers: the gap between how you think you write and how you actually write, the writing you admire vs. the writing that comes naturally, the situations where your voice changes and why.

Examples of what deeper questions sound like:

- *Walk me through how you write an email when you're asking for something you're not sure you'll get. What's your instinct?*
- *Tell me about a piece of writing you've done that you're proud of. What made it work?*
- *What's a word or phrase in professional writing that makes you wince when you see it — even if you can't fully explain why?*
- *If you had to describe your communication style in three adjectives, what would they be? Now cross out the one that's most aspirational rather than true.*
- *When you write to someone more senior, what changes? Is it conscious?*
- *Read back something I've written for you recently. What's wrong with it?*
- *Is there a piece of writing — anything, not just professional — that you wish you'd written? What does it do that you want to do?*
- *What do you think people expect from your writing that you don't always deliver? What do you actually deliver that they don't expect?*

These questions don't have right or wrong answers. What Claude is listening for is the texture of how you think about your own voice — the contradictions, the ideals, the habits you've noticed and the ones you haven't. A full profile built this way can run to 20–30 pages of Q&A, but even a single deeper session produces something more useful than the short version alone.

If you want to do this, start a dedicated Cowork session with no other agenda. Tell Claude you want to build a voice profile, that you want it to ask questions and follow up, and that you'll answer as honestly as you can. The session can run as long as it needs to.

**If you build a full profile, use a tiered reading rule.** A deep profile can run to thousands of words, and reading it before every two-line email is wasted effort. Add this to your planning session instructions: read `voice-quick-ref.md` for routine drafting (emails, short documents); read the full profile only for high-stakes writing (cover letters, grant text, anything substantial or career-relevant). The quick-ref handles the everyday; the profile earns its length on the writing that matters.
